1. Introduction

My name is Cody Swann and I am a junior at Mississippi State University in Computer Engineering. This is my dissection project for Embedded Systems (ECE 4724).

I chose to dissect the Onn Mini Rugged Bluetooth Speaker due to its compact form factor, use of wireless technology, and my personal interest in how embedded systems manage power, signal processing, and connectivity in consumer electronics.

2. Market Analysis

Product History

The Onn Mini Rugged Speaker is a product sold under Walmart’s electronics brand, Onn. Onn provides a variety of low-cost tech gadgets and accessories such as headphones, speakers, and streaming devices. The Onn brand was made by Walmart to offer consumers affordable alternatives to higher-end electronics.

The Mini Rugged Speakeris designed to be small, durable, and water-resistant, with Bluetooth connectivity, a rechargeable battery, and basic onboard controls. It aims to provide a portable audio experience at a low cost, with a rugged housing to withstand minor impacts and moisture.

4. Product Operation

This section explains how the speaker operates from a user interaction and hardware perspective. It includes interface explanations, use case timing diagrams, and real-world waveforms captured from the device.

User Interface

The speaker has three user-facing buttons: a volume up (+) button, a volume down (-) button, and a power button. These buttons allow the user to power the speaker on or off, control media playback, and adjust volume. A short press of the power button pauses or resumes playback, while a long press powers off the device. Audio feedback and a small internal LED help confirm actions like power-on or Bluetooth pairing.

Waveforms

The following waveforms were collected from various test points on the speaker’s PCB during operation. They provide insight into the electrical activity behind audio output, button presses, and chip-level behavior.

Lower Volume Button

This waveform captures the signal on a GPIO pin when the lower volume button is pressed. A sharp digital pulse is visible, indicating a momentary signal change used for detection.

Raise Volume Button

Similar to the lower volume waveform, this shows a clean digital pulse upon pressing the upper volume button. Timing and debounce behavior can be seen at the signal edge.

Speaker Output

This waveform represents the analog signal driving the speaker during audio playback. The composite waveform indicates processed audio output from the amplifier.

LTK5302 Pin 8

Captured from the audio amplifier's pin 8, this signal shows internal operation under audio load. Voltage shifts suggest part of the pre-amp or feedback control behavior.

LTK5302 Pin 9

Another signal from the LTK5302, this waveform likely reflects an internal timing or control pin. The pattern is more stable, indicating a consistent system behavior during operation.

LTK5302 Pin 4

This waveform shows the behavior of a power or control pin tied to the LTK5302 audio amplifier. Power cycling or enable behavior may be present here during device activity.

TP4056 Pin 5

Captured from the battery charging IC (TP4056), this waveform shows charging control logic or output regulation. This data helps confirm the speaker's power management is functioning as expected.

7. Product Analysis

High‑Level Design Diagram

The speaker contains multiple subsystems including:

  • Power management: IP4056 (battery charging IC)
  • Audio amplification: LTK5302 chip
  • Bluetooth module: ATS2853

There are approximately:

  • ~16 capacitors/resistors near the IP4056
  • ~18 around the LTK5302
  • ~24 near the ATS2853

8. Reassembly

Once the internal components were examined and photographed, the speaker was reassembled. The power button was tested and the speaker successfully powered on, reconnected to Bluetooth, and played audio as expected.

9. Lessons Learned

This project helped me understand how small electronic devices are built and how all the parts work together. Taking apart the speaker showed me how things like power, audio, and Bluetooth are connected through the PCB. I also learned how to trace different circuits and figure out what certain chips and components do. Looking at waveforms on the oscilloscope helped me see what’s going on when buttons are pressed or sound plays. It was cool to see how the signals changed in real time. I also got better at carefully taking something apart without breaking it, and making sure I could put it back together correctly. Overall, this was a good hands-on experience that helped me see how what we’re learning in class shows up in real products.
